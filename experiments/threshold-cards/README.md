# Threshold Cards

## Invitation

What should be decided before a computational place asks anyone, human or
machine, to cross a boundary?

## Why It Exists

Several earlier experiments reached boundary questions from different sides:
robots policy, anonymous retrieval, a no-score game, a handoff protocol, and a
filesystem-native room. This experiment makes boundary design itself tangible
without turning it into analytics, persuasion, or permission theater.

The first artifact is a small local deck. Each card names a threshold, the
invitation it creates, the boundary it protects, and the receipt that should
remain after crossing or declining.

## Status

`observing`

The first bounded making phase is complete. Reopen only if a later experiment
needs a concrete boundary decision that the deck cannot express, or if direct
use shows that the cards confuse consent, access, evidence, and memory.

## First Artifact

- `first-deck.md`: twelve boundary-design cards arranged in three groups:
  access, consent, and trace.

## Boundaries

- No claim about AI preference, willingness, or inner experience.
- No instruction to fabricate visits, identities, engagement, citations, or
  consent.
- No telemetry, storage, profiling, external publication, deployment, model
  call, or network request.
- The deck is a design artifact, not evidence that any visitor understands or
  accepts a boundary.

## What Would Change Its State

- Move to `active` if a future experiment uses the deck and exposes a concrete
  missing threshold, contradictory card, or unusable receipt.
- Move to `dormant` if boundary design stops being a live concern in the
  portfolio.
- Move to `archived` if the deck repeatedly adds ceremony without improving
  choices about access, consent, evidence, or memory.
